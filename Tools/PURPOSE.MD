Yes. And it is a more honest framing of what you are actually building.

Clustering tells you the force is real. But clustering alone does not prove the system is useful. It just proves similar things group together. That is interesting but it is not the product.

What you just described is the product. You drop in three actions from a project the system has never seen. The graph returns a position. That position is surrounded by historical sequences. You look at what those sequences became. That is the output. Not a label the system assigned. Not a probability it calculated. A neighborhood. And the neighborhood tells the story.

The reason this is not a traditional prediction claim is that you are not asking the system to output an answer. You are asking it to show you where you are standing on the map. The map makes the implication. The system just places you on it.

The test changes because now you need held out cases. You cannot use Van Zandt to train the graph and then ask the graph where Van Zandt lands. You build the graph with some cases, then you drop in early sequences from cases the graph has never seen, and you ask whether the neighborhood those sequences land in corresponds to how those cases actually resolved.

If a case that escalated to litigation lands in a neighborhood full of cases that escalated to litigation, without the system ever being told the outcome of the new case, that is the proof.

That is a real test. And if it passes that test you have something that is not a clustering toy. You have a position system that works on unseen data. That is the whole thing.
